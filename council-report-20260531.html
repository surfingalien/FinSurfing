Where the Council Agrees

Supervisor averaging is architecturally wrong
30-day stale timer is broken
Price targets create false precision
Feedback loop is missing + is the moat
Explainability drives retention

The Supervisor is architecturally wrong. Every advisor converged on this independently. Averaging 5 agent scores destroys the signal. When Technical scores 72 and Sentiment scores 31, the 41-point spread IS the output — not a 51.5 composite. The Supervisor must surface contradictions, not smooth them.

Stale thesis detection by calendar is broken. A 30-day timer on a thesis is noise. Theses expire when specific assumptions are violated, not when time passes. The assumption needs to be the instrument, not the clock.

Price targets as precise numbers are a liability. "$147.23 entry" implies certainty LLM agents provably cannot provide. Show confidence zones or ranges instead.

The feedback loop is the core moat. AI Brain predictions are never validated against outcomes. Without closing the loop, there is no mechanism for the system to improve and no advantage over a free ChatGPT prompt. This must be built deliberately.

Explainability drives trust more than accuracy. "Sentiment dropped 18 points because three analyst downgrades hit this morning" is more valuable to retention than any architectural upgrade.

Where the Council Clashes

Personalization vs. base layer first. The Expansionist argues for per-user intelligence — the system learns that a momentum trader should weight Technical+Sentiment over Fundamental. The peer reviews consistently flagged this as premature: cold-start kills it. New users have no behavioral history. The Expansionist's vision is the correct long-term destination; it's the wrong Monday-morning move.

Is unit economics a veto? The Contrarian argues 300 API calls for a 50-symbol scan makes the business unviable. Other advisors largely ignored this. It is a real constraint but a solvable engineering problem (caching, tiered scans, batching) — not a product direction veto.

Price targets: remove or reframe? The Contrarian says remove entirely. The Outsider and Executor say keep with confidence ranges. First Principles says reframe to zones. The right answer: if users are retail traders who execute, removing targets eliminates why they opened the app. Reframing to zones with explicit uncertainty is the correct middle path.

Blind Spots the Council Caught

Explainability as a retention mechanism. The most architecturally perfect system is a black box if users can't read the reasoning. "Why did Fundamental score this 72 vs 45?" needs a human-readable answer attached to every output. This is the difference between a tool users return to and one they abandon after two sessions.

Behavioral override data as ground truth. When a user ignores a high-conviction call, or overrides a stop-loss suggestion, that is behavioral ground-truth about what users actually believe vs. what the system output. This data should be captured from day one.

The competitive moat question. Why pay for FinSurfing's AI Brain when Perplexity or ChatGPT produces similar qualitative output for near-zero cost? The answer is accumulated user context and outcome tracking — but that moat only exists if you build it deliberately. Free alternatives cannot know your specific thesis history or which setups worked for your style.

Data quality as a ceiling. SEC EDGAR ingestion (free API, two days of work) gives the Fundamental agent a primary source it currently lacks. 10-K/10-Q parsing immediately upgrades thesis quality.

The Recommendation

Rebuild the Supervisor as a contradiction engine, instrument every prediction against outcomes, and make the reasoning legible.

Supervisor as contradiction engine: When agents disagree significantly (25+ point spread between any two agents), that spread becomes the primary output. A stock where Technical=78 and Fundamental=29 is not a 53 — it is "technically extended beyond what fundamentals support; momentum trade with tight stop, not an investment." That framing is actually useful.

Instrument every prediction: Log every agent score, every Supervisor synthesis, every price target zone at the moment of generation. Capture actual price movement at 7/30/90-day intervals. Display win rates per agent, per sector, per market condition. Start capturing now — everything else depends on this data existing.

Make the reasoning legible: Every score must have a two-sentence plain-language explanation. Not bullet points — prose. "Sentiment is 31 because three institutional analysts downgraded the stock in the past week and social volume dropped 40%."

Second Brain — assumption tracking: Replace the 30-day timer with explicit assumption monitoring. When a user writes a thesis, extract 3–5 falsifiable assumptions. Each becomes a monitoring target against live data. When violated, the thesis is flagged — not because 30 days passed, but because the world changed.

Technical analysis: Connect TA signals to the user's existing thesis. "Your bull thesis requires volume confirmation on breakout — volume today is 40% below average, the signal is incomplete" is worth more than any RSI reading. The TA agent should be aware of what the user's thesis requires.

The One Thing to Do First

Instrument every AI Brain output with a timestamp and the stock's forward price, then build the simplest possible win-rate display.

Every other improvement on this list requires data that does not yet exist. Add a logging table capturing agent scores, Supervisor output, and price target zones at generation time. Add a daily job that fetches actual prices at 7/30/90-day marks. Add a single "Win Rate" display showing, per agent, how often the signal direction was correct. Two days of work. Everything else — the Supervisor contradiction engine, personalization, assumption-violation triggers, per-user learning — becomes dramatically easier once that loop is closed.

Full Advisor Responses

⚡ The Contrarian
The fundamental problem with "5 parallel agents synthesizing price targets" is that it's expensive theater. Running 6 Claude calls per stock scan — if a broad scan hits 50 symbols, you're burning 300 API calls. The unit economics almost certainly don't support a price point retail traders will pay. The price target output is also a liability, not a differentiator. When Claude's "Supervisor agent" says $147 entry and the stock gaps down 15%, you own that failure. Second Brain's 30-day stale detection reveals deeper design confusion — theses expire when assumptions break, not on a schedule. The Finnhub/FMP data layer is your actual ceiling. If technical analysis agents run on adjusted price data with survivorship bias, every conviction score is fiction.
🔬 The First Principles Thinker
Users pay for better decisions with less regret — not more information. A retail investor lacks confidence at the moment of trade execution. The synthesis layer is wrong: a Supervisor averaging scores is averaging opinions. Real conviction comes from disagreement resolution — when Technical says 72 and Sentiment says 31, that tension IS the signal. Build the Supervisor to surface and explain contradictions. Thesis staleness should be tied to assumption violations, not time. Technical analysis is a commodity — the differentiation is connecting TA signals to the specific thesis a user already holds. "Your bull thesis requires volume confirmation — it's not there yet" is infinitely more valuable than a generic RSI reading.
🚀 The Expansionist
The real opportunity is a compounding intelligence layer that gets smarter the longer users stay. The Supervisor should run a meta-learning loop — track which agent's signals preceded actual price moves for each user's portfolio style. Second Brain is a private knowledge graph: connect theses across symbols, add cross-thesis contradiction detection. Add pattern memory to TA — when a setup forms, retrieve the last 10 times this exact pattern appeared in similar macro conditions, with outcomes. Each feature creates lock-in through accumulated user-specific intelligence Robinhood cannot replicate.
👁 The Outsider
Three things jump out: (1) Data latency visibility — show "this score was generated from data as of X minutes ago." (2) The braindump-to-thesis flow is solving the wrong problem. Retail investors don't struggle to write their thesis — they struggle to know when it was wrong from the start. Second Brain should aggressively surface counterarguments, not just structure beliefs. (3) Price targets create false precision. "Entry: $147.23" implies certainty LLM agents cannot provide. Show confidence zones. If FinSurfing differentiates on research depth, it needs to make uncertainty visible, not hide it behind clean numbers.
⚙️ The Executor
Monday-morning moves ranked by impact-per-hour: (1) Confidence intervals on agent scores. (2) Surface inter-agent disagreement signals explicitly. (3) Cache Finnhub/FMP calls per symbol per 15-min window. (4) SEC EDGAR ingestion — free API, 10-K/10-Q parsing, two days of work. (5) "What changed" diff on stale thesis re-analysis. (6) Volume profile for the TA agent. (7) Backtest entry/target/stop outputs against 30-day outcomes — even a win-rate display builds trust. First step: instrument current agent outputs to capture prediction vs actual outcome. Everything else depends on having that ground truth.
📋 Peer Review Highlights

All 5 reviewers: First Principles (D) named strongest by 4/5 reviewers. Expansionist (A) named biggest blind spot by all 5 — cold-start problem: per-user weighting requires months of data retail users won't generate before churning.

What all missed — consensus across reviews:

  • Feedback loop as foundational requirement — no mechanism to improve without outcome tracking
  • Plain-language explainability as retention mechanism, not UX nicety
  • Behavioral override data (user ignores high-conviction call) as ground truth signal
  • Competitive moat question vs. free AI alternatives
